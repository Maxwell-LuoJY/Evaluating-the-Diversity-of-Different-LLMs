The scent of rain-soaked earth, known as petrichor, is a surprisingly complex phenomenon.  While it might seem like a simple earthy aroma, it's actually a combination of plant oils, a soil-dwelling bacterium, and ozone. During dry periods, plants release oils into the soil, and a bacterium called Streptomyces produces geosmin, a strong earthy-smelling compound. When rain falls, it releases these trapped scents into the air, along with ozone, which is created by lightning or the interaction of sunlight with car emissions.  The result is that distinct, fresh, and almost sweet smell we associate with the first rain after a dry spell.
